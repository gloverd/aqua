Video 2(B) - Cell Membrane and Structure

The main parts of the cell:

  • Mitochondria create ATP in the cell. The Rough endoplasmic reticulum synthesises, stores, then releases proteins for use inside or outside the cell. Most important to us is the plasma membrane, it divides the inside from the outside of the cell.

The cell membrane is a lipid bylayer that is made up of polar molecules. the lipid tails (hydrophobic) face each other in the interior of the membrane. The polar head groups (hydrophillic) face outside, and there are several different molecules but they have polar heads and lipid tails.

The lipid tails internal to the membrane there are other structures associated with the membrane Integral proteins are proteins that exist throughout the membrane (through and through). There are peripheral proteins that are on the inside of the cell, and carbohydrates that exist on the outside of the cells.

Cell Channels or proteins are made up of portions of protein that traverse the bilayer. segments that are embedded in the membrane (non-polar portions of the protein, they dissolve well), other portions are in the extracellular or intracellular region where they have polar segments.

The basic channel is made up of proteins of Four Segments, this is a subunit, and one of those four segments is slightly different. if 5 segments unit together, with the different section pointing inwards that faces the interior they can be a channel that can be specialized for different ions.

Video 3(C) - Membrane Transport

Transport mechanisms, are:

  • Diffusion - (Simple diffusion) - moves from higher concentration down to lower concentration, doesn't show specificity, saturation, or competition.
  • Facilitated diffusion - requires a a carrier or transport protein to be embedded in the membrane. Moves from high concentration to low concentration. shows specificity, saturation and competition. If there is a channel then it is facilitated diffusion.
  • Active transport - moves against the gradient and moves from a segment of low concentration into high concentration. This requires ATP or energy. displays specificity, saturation, competition, and metabolic inhibition.
  • Diffusion over small distances takes a short amount of time. Over a long distance it takes exponential amounts of time.
  • Transport of molecules (endocytosis and exocytosis), where vesicles fuse with the cell membrane, and discharge the material, or invaginations form and create vesicles.
  • Channels are Open or Closed, in their simplest forms. They can be somewhat selective, as for a class of substances (double charged cations) or super selective (sodium channels).

Video 4(D) - Osmosis and osmotic pressure

A cell of a certain concentration (conc) of non penetrating solutes, in the following solutions:

  • Isotonic solution - same conc of non penetrating solutes, the cell stays the same volume
  • Hypertonic solution - larger conc of non penetrating solutes, the cell shrinks.
  • Hypotonic solution - smaller conc of non penetrating solutes, the cell swells. you will reach a point where the cell will burst (lysis).
  • Excessive shrinking or swelling, will stress the membrane and disturb the function of the membrane proteins. An isotonic solution is required to maintain homeostasis.

Osmotic pressure can be derived from:

osmotic pressure(mmHg) = R*T*\phi*i*c
R=62.36 L*mmHg*degK^-1*mol^-1
T=absolute Temp, K
/phi = osmotic coefficient
i=number of particles (no account of size/charge)
c=molar concentration, mol*L^-1 (molar, moles/liter)

and Osmolality is:

Osmolality = \phi*n*c
/phi = osmotic coefficient
n=number of particles
c=molal concentration (mol solute/kg solvent)

154nM NaCl is isotonic to human red blood cells.

Video 5(E) - Cellular Receptors / Binding sites

Ligands bind to receptors on the cells; the shapes fit and the charge distribution matches creating tight binding (high affinity). How is this done?

  • There are portions of the protein that make up a structure, and due to their folding they make up polar(charged) and non polar (non charged) three dimensional shapes that when put together with ligands match. Multiple ligands are able to bind to receptors, but sometimes they can be very specific. the more general the shape and charge distribution the more likely it is to be able to bind to multiple receptors/ligands.
  • high affinity binding, fits charge and shape. intermediate affinity, is a weak shape or week charge binding. low affinity, is ill fitting shape or no charge.
  • Allosteric modulation is when there is a regulatory site remote from the functional binding site, where if a regulatory molecule binds to it, it will modify the functional site, and then the ligand can bind.
  • Covalent modulation is when phosphorylation can happen on a remote site (say -OH) the energy from hydrolysis of ATP changes the functional site. dephosphorylation will revert the change. This happens with smooth muscle.

Video 2(B) - Ion Channels

The structure of a voltage dependent sodium channel is made out of four repeating 6-unit motifs. The channel is not just a cylinder, but the position of the amino acids in the channel and their composition will determine its functionality.

  • The S4 of each unit is the voltage sensor which detects membrane potential, determining the opening/closing of the channel.
  • The intracellular loop connecting domains 3-4 is the inactivation gate. (Think ball-and-chain method).
  • Helix that connects 5-6 in each of the domains, it dips back into the membrane as a "pore loop". that helps form the selectivity filter of the channel. The residues in the loop determine the ionic selectivity of the channel.

A potassium channel, and other channels, work as transistors. over a range of voltages, it has an ON or OFF status, but the switch from one to the other happens over a very short period (more sensitive). Channels are Quantal in nature (OPEN or CLOSED).

Measuring Membrane Potential can be done with Cell-attached mode which is where a pipette is used to patch clamp to hopefully only one channel. An Inside out patch, where the membrane is ripped along with the pore is ripped out of the membrane, so you can control solutions on each side (pipette and solution imersed in). Finally Whole cell mode you suck it open to control the whole inside of the cell, compared to the outside, and control the solution itself.

Electrotonic conduction - if a depolarization occurs over a region of a cell membrane there is a spread of depolarization. There is current between the deploarized and resting (because of a change in voltage). Every patch of cell has to be depolarized in a wave. As the flow moves along the axon, size of the depolarization decreases. The action potential will eventually die out over time, the depolarization will decay at 1/e.

for a length diameter (lambda) a large neuron has low resistance there is a certain kind of fall off; on a smaller diameter there is a higher resistance, and the space constant is smaller, so the effect of the sub action potential falls off faster.

schwann cells wrap around a nerve cell to provide insulation for the nerve. These form myelin sheaths, which wrap around a large insulation. They are separated by sections of uninsulated nerve, called Nodes of Ranvier. The current of electrotonic conduction will not happen on each adjacent patch, since it is wrapped by the insulator on the internode-regions. it jumps from node to node forming saltatory conduction. Fast local currents happen inside the myelin sheaths (internodes), and the action potentials jump from node to node. The velocity is distance/time, and it is a high conduction velocity in the internode region.

Video 4(D) - Chemical Synapses

Nerve anatomy, three salient features:

  • Cell body - also called the Soma
  • dendrites - carry incomming signals into the cell body. extend volumetrically all around the cell body.
  • axon - one outgoing process, carries efferent signal. these make contact all along the nerve.
  • pre-synaptic terminals, ends of axises. these overlay forming synapses.
  • synapse - where pre-synaptic terminals overlay the neuron.

Pre-synaptic terminals lie close to but not touching the post-synaptic membrane, forming the synapse with the space between being called the synaptic cleft. the post-synaptic terminal has receptor proteins on the surface.

An action potential that is fired on the axon of a pre-synaptic cell processes down the axon down to the terminal bouton (pre-synaptic terminal). this openes voltage gated Ca2+ channels. This allows vesicles carrying neurotransmitters, to fuse with the membrane and diffuse them across the synaptic cleft and bind to post-synaptic receptors. These neurotransmitters are then removed from the synaptic cleft either drifts away, is re-uptaken, or recycled (or broken down by enzymes).

When a neurotransmitter binds to a receptor, and the receptor affects/open a channel it can have the following responses that are rather local to the region of the synapse:

  • ** Effectatory post-synaptic potential (EPSP)** - say some sodium was allowed to enter the channel. it did not reach threshold to trigger an action potential, but it depolarized the membrane, this can die out over time/distance as mentioned above.
  • ** Inhibitory post-synaptic potential (IPSP)** - If it is a potassium channel, where the exit of potassium will cause a hyperpolarization (polarization of the channel).

These can be added together in the following ways:

  • Temporal Summation -- One signal is triggered twice very fast.
    • Think: ----A-----A-A-A-------
  • Spatial Summation - Multiple signals at the same time, that add together.
    • Think: --A-----B-----AB--------AB

A combination of both can happen, to have signals sent at the same time/fast. An inhibitory synapse can also negate these effects using the same summation, meaning it is not only positive additions. There are thousands and thousands of these synapses on each post-synaptic cell. Distance from the Soma is a a factor during summation.

If excitatory inputs are sent from far away of the cell body, they can be cancelled easily by inhibitory signals close to the cell body. The summation actually happens at an area called the Axon hillock it is the initial segment of the axon closest to the cell body. It is very rich in sodium channels, so if an action potential is fired, it is fired there. and then travels down the Axon.

Video 2(B) - Structure

  • A muscle is composed of bundles of muscle fasciculus (made up of individual fasciculi) which is made up of what appear to be cylinders. These are Muscle Fibers. Each Muscle Fiber cell contains multiple myofibrils, which have banding patterns on them. This gives it the name Striated Muscle.
  • Bands are called sarcomeres (delineated by Z-disks) which is a functional unit of skeletal muscle. There are various regions within this band. The sarcomeres are composed of proteins, Actin and myosin, as well as titin which is used to hold things in place, but does not play a direct role in contractility. The thin filaments are arranged in a cross section so that there is a regular hexagonal array. Thick filaments have 6 nearest neighbors, thin filaments have 3.
    • A sarcomere is delineated by Z-disks on the sides. Between the two disks are various bands. These are characteristic of what filaments are where and what is overlapping.
    • The H-band is at the center of the sarcomere (called the M-line) and is composed of only myosin. It is dark because myosin is thick but not the darkest. The A-band is also centered within the sarcomere, and has the H-band overlapping its center. This band is the full width of the myosin filaments. On each of the ends of the A-band's (that is the section that is not overlapping with the H-band), is where myosin and actin overlap.
    • The I-band is centered over the z-disk itself, so each extreme of a sarcomere has a portion of an I-band. These are composed only of the thin filament.
  • Actin (thin filament) - From the z-disks projecting to the center of the sarcomere.
    • Actin is a globular protein (can be monomeric) which aggregates together to form a filament. Two of these filaments wound around each other form an actin thin filament. The protein nebulin (structural protein) aligns the thin filament and keeps it in register - no other function or impact in contractility.
    • Two other proteins Tropomyosin (rod like) and troponin (more globular).
  • Myosin (thick filament) - From the center of the sarcomere projecting outwards, held in place by titin. Light myosin and Heavy myosin exist within it.
    • Have a swelling at one end (The S1 region, composed of light chains) but otherwise rod like (heavy chains, insoluble tail portion). They aggregate tail to tail at the central region of the sarcomere. The cross projections (also called cross-bridges) are spaced at regular longitudinal and circumferential intervals.
    • The cross bridges have an S2-segment which acts as a coiled spring, and an S1-segment which has two heads, but functionally can be thought of as one. The S1 globular heads are important for the function of myosin ATPase activity. A hinge region exists between S1-S2 and at the connection to the tail-S1. These give the filament a large range of motion in which to move.
  • Troponin has three distinct sub-units -- TnC, TnI, TnT.
Video 3(C) - Some Observations on Force
  • During contraction, the A-band length doesn't change. Invariant to sarcomere length. The I-band gets smaller as I-disks get pushed together. The H-zone will get smaller, and in some cases can vanish. Suggests a sliding mechanism, where filaments themselves do not change length.
  • Force Generation over sarcomere length has a plateau where force is optimal, while sarcomere length changes.
    • At 3.65 micron length of the sarcomere there is no tension generated. no filament overlap.
    • At 2.62 there is maximal overlap. Force increases from 3.65 until 2.62 in a linear manner.
    • Until 2.0 there the force plateau, where maximal strength is contained.
    • Until 1.62 there is a decrease where the thin filaments overlap and there are less cross bridges connected between actin and myosin.
    • Until 1.27 there is a very sharp decrease which is attributed to the overlap of one extreme of myosin reaching the opposing myosin filament, which then 'pushes it' instead of pulls since the overlap is so far. and it works to accelerate the decrease in force generation. Finally the interactions have no other movement to do.

StepNotes
1AP down the motor nerve propagates down the motor neuron until it reaches the pre-synaptic terminal.
2The action potential on the pre-synaptic terminal allows the entry of calcium through voltage gated channels.
3The calcium allows the transmitter vesicles to fuse with the membrane of the pre-synaptic terminal. In skeletal muscle this is Acetylcholine (ACh).
4It crosses the synaptic cleft to the end plate, and attaches to AcH receptors, which opens Na+ Channels, which depolarizes the muscle cell membrane to threshold.
5This local depolarization travels to the adjacent muscle plasma membrane
6Travels down t-tubules which then releases Ca from the SR...
7There are ACh-esterases in the muscle cell membrane which degrade ACh so that the receptors become unblocked, some is difussed away, and some is taken back up into the pre-synaptic terminal.
  • The Motor End Plate has lots of Sodium channels and the AcH-esterases.
  • The whole process looks as follows:

  1. Action potential is initiated and propagates to motor neuron axon terminals.

  2. Calcium enters axon terminals through voltage-gated calcium channels.

  3. Calcium entry triggers release of ACh from axon terminals.

  4. ACh diffuses from axon terminals to motor end plate in muscle fiber.

  5. ACh binds to nicotinic receptors on motor end plate, increasing their permeability to Na+ and K+.

  6. More Na+ moves into the fiber at the motor end plate that K+ moves out, depolarizing the membrane and producing the end plate potential (EPP).

  7. Local currents depolarize the adjacent muscle cell plasma membrane to its threshold potential, generation an action potential that propagates over the muscle fiber surface and into the fiber along the t-tubules.

  8. Action potential in T-tubules induces DHP receptors to pull open RyR channels allowing release of Ca2+ from lateral sacs of sarcoplasmic reticulum.

  9. Ca2+ binds to troponin on the thin filaments, causing tropomyosin to move away from its blocking position thereby uncovering cross bridge binding sites on actin.

  10. Energized myosin cross-bridges on the thick filaments bind to actin:

    A + M*ADP*Pi -> A*M*ADP*Pi

  11. Cross-bridge binding triggers release of ATP hydrolysis products from myosin, producing an angular movement of each cross-bridge:

    A*M*ADP*Pi -> A*M + ADP + Pi

  12. ATP binds to myosin, breaking linkage between actin and myosin and thereby allowing cross-bridges to dissociate from actin:

    A*M + ATP -> A + M*ATP

  13. ATP bound to myosin is split, energizing the myosin cross-bridge:

    A + M*ATP -> A + M*ADP*Pi

  14. Cross-bridges repeat steps 10-13, producing movement (sliding) of the thin filaments past thick filaments. Cycles of cross-bridge movement continue as long as Ca2+ remains bound to troponin.

  15. Cytosolic Ca2+ concentration decreases as Ca2+-APTase actively transports Ca2+ into sarcoplasmic reticulum.

  16. Removal of Ca2+ from troponin restores blocking action of tropomyosin, the cross-bridge cycle ceases, and the muscle fiber relaxes.

Module 7(G) - Mechanics

  • If a muscle length is held constant a contraction is called Isometric contraction. After a stimulus, the muscle develops force, reaches a peak and then falls.
  • If the muscle is allowed to contract and move a load with its shortening, then it is called an Isotonic Contraction. With this contraction there is a latent period while it gathers sufficient force to lift the weight. there is a peak amount of shortening, the muscle develops force, and then a rapid lenghening process untill the load is dropped back onto its support.
  • Force vs Time - and isometric twitch there is no length change. For isotonic twitches, the muscles will increase tension (climb isometric envelope) until it is able to lift the load, then it will shorten.
  • The initial velocity of shortening (V_0) is higher for light loads than for heavy loads. As load increases V_0 it goes through a hyperbola shape until the load is the same as the force and there is no isotonic shortening (zero velocity).
  • When V_0 is maximal, load on the muscle is 0, is a reflection of the cycling rate of the cross bridges which in turn is a reflection of the ATPase rate in the muscle.
  • A ** Lengthening contraction** is when the lengthening velocity is negative.
  • A muscle fiber AP (3ms) is rapid compared to contraction (200ms). if there is a faster rate of stimuli there can be a faster response to stimuli and an unfused tetanus can occur, a relatively constant force with some oscilations. If the stimulation rate is fast enough you can get a fused tetanus which is on the order of 3X the force of a single twitch. This is because intracellular free [Ca2+] increaes each time, and oscilates with the stimulation and reaches a fairly constant value.
  • Another way to increase force, is to increase the number of muscle cells firing together. More motor units can fire together at the same time. Very fine control muscles (orbital muscles of the eye), have small number of muscles per motor units, so control is at a small quanta. Postural muscles have a small number of muscle units, but many fibers per motor unit.
  • Asynchronos motor-unit activity can maintain a near constant tension in the total muscle. Staggering the twitch contractions of individual motor units can be effective instead of firing one unit many times.

    • Muscle contraction and relaxation requires energy for maintainance of resting membrane potential (Na+/K+ pump in the sarcolemma of every cell), and without that pump it won't be electrically excitable.
    • Energy is required to form energized Myosin (M*ATP -> M*ADP*Pi)
    • Update of Ca2+ into the SR, to reduce intracellular [Ca2+] to relax the muscle.

If the rate increases the muscle will fatigue, even though the APs continue to trigger, but we have run out of ATP. If a muscle is tenanized it will fatigue, after a small rest it will be able to tenatize but for not as long then fatigue again.

Module 4 - Smooth Muscle

Video 1(A) - Introduction

  • Overview Similarities - Both smooth muscle cells (SMC) and skeletal muscle use same contractile mechanism (the interaction of Actin and Myosin). Both use intracellular [Ca2+] to contract/relax. The peak force in smooth muscle and skeletal muscle are about the same, even though sises are the same.
  • Overview Differences - Smooth muscles are smaller and shaped differently. Smooth muscles are autonomic, instead of somatic, and are not voluntary. The site of regulation of contraction is at the thick filaments, not thin (skeletal). The time course for smooth muscle is slower than skeletal muscle. There are no t-tubules in smooth muscle, and the SMC have a higher degree of shortening (proportionally) than skeletal muscle, so the Force Length Curve is broader in SMC than Skeletal.

Video 2(B) - Anatomy

  • Skeletal muscle cells (Myofibrils) - long and cylindrical, with a ratio of length:diameter is large. 100microns diameter, several mm long.
  • Smooth muscle cells (SMC) - more amorphous with tapering at either end (Spindle shaped), less ratio of length:diameter. 2-10 microns in diameter, 40-600micros long.
  • SMCs have Dense Bodies which serve as anchor points for intermediate filaments and thin filaments. There are Gap Junctions have electrical and chemical communication between cells. There are thin fillaments and thick filaments that form sarcomeres. But these are at different angles along the cell so there is not a uniform pattern.
  • 2x Actin and .25x Myosin compared to skeletal muscle. When they contract since they are not held at the ends, they kind of ball up, and form a 'netting' over the muscle.

Video 1(A) - Cardiac Action Potentials : Types and what they look like (10:28)

  1. Slow (pacemaker) action potential -- found in specialized regions in the heart (e.g. the sino atrial node and the atrial ventricular node)

    • Has phases 0 (rise),3 (fall), 4 (afterhyperpolarization).
    • resting membrane potential ~-60 mV, peak at positive, quick return to 'resting' which is a slow re-polarization.
    • duration ~200ms

  2. Fast action potential - found in cardiac muscle.

    • 0 (rapid upstroke), 1 (rapid early repolarization), 2 (droopy plateau region), 3 (recovery (repolarization) region), 4 (return to resting, at resting)
    • resting membrate potential ~-80 mV
    • duration ~200ms

Cardiac fast action potential details - The duration of the action potential extends well into the contraction event (force/contraction/shortening event), which means you cannot tetanize cardiac muscle. There is a very sharp phase 0, phase 1 isn't strong, phase 2 is very droopy and hybrid with phase 3, then return to resting (4). The shape looks different than the idealized shape. It is expected because it is different in several parts of the heart. This depends on what channels are in the membranes of the heart. For example:

  • Atrial tissue is droopy phase 2, and merged w/ 3.
  • purkinje fibers show a less droppy phase 2
  • Endocardial shows similar to above
  • Midmyocardias shows a large plateau that is flat for phase 2
  • Epicardial, has a rather short plateau, and a faster fall.
  • Sino atrial (SA nodal), and atrial ventricular (AV nodal) regions you see the slow slow pacemaker action potential shapes.

  • Phase 0 - (rapid upstroke from negative resting potential to somewhat positive value)
    • Sodium inrush current, [Na+]_o is high and interior of the cell is negative w.r.t the outside.
    • both the chemical and electrostatic forces both favor the entry of Na+ into the cell through fast Na+ channels to generate the upstroke.
    • negative interior
  • Phase 1 - (Initial repolarization)
    • Depends on potassium efflux. There is a fast (i_to) transient outward K+ current, which accounts for this early phase. How prominent this is depends on the density of the channels that conduct i_to in the particular caridac cell.
    • chemical and electrical forces both favor the efflux of K+ through i_to channels to generate early, partial repolarization.
    • positive interior
  • Phase 2 - (plateau phase)
    • Balance between inflow of Ca2+ down its concentration gradient and an efflux of K+. The voltage is a bit positive or a bit negative, so electrical drive is not very strong, but chemical (concentration) will determine.
    • Depending on conductivity of various Ca and K channels, it will determine if it droops a bit and how long it will last.
    • During the plateau, the net influx of Ca++ through Ca++ channels is balanced by the efflux of K+ through i_K, i_K1, and i_to channels.
    • positive interior
  • Phase 3 - (recovery/repolarization period)
    • Calcium channels have closed, so primarily determined by which of the several K channels permit efflux.
    • The chemical forces that favor the efflux of K+ through i_k and i_k1 channels predominate over the electrostatic forces that favor the influx of K+ through these same channels.
    • chemical out, electrical in for K
    • negative interior.
  • Phase 4 - (resting potential)
    • slight K+ efflux during baseline.
    • The chemical forces that favor the efflux of K+ thorugh I_k, i_k1 channels very slightly exceed the electrostatic forces that favor the influx of K+ through these same channels.
    • chemical out, electrical in for K
    • negative interior

Small background on heart anatomy. The heart is divided into left/right by the steptum (interatrial and interventricular) and top/bottom. Divided left/right at the bottom of the heart are the Ventricles which are the main pumping chambers of the heart. on the top are the Atriums. The left portion of the heart is the right side of the heart*. AN easy way to remember this is to view it from your point of view facing out.

Pathway of blood:

  • Superior vena cava - brings blood to heart from upper part of body (arms chest head)

  • Inferior vena cava - brings blood to heart from lower parts of body (legs)

  • Ventricles

    • These deposit blood into the right atrium. Near here there is a specialized cells that fire the pacemaker action potential Sinoatrial node.
    • Because the cells are in a synsnctium (?) they conduct to the left atrium, and bring the action potential to the atrio ventricular node, in the bottom center of the right atrium, near the septum. The node has conducting cells called the bundle of His, and it splits bundles down the lower ventricles inside the ventricles for the left and right branch through what is called Purkinje fibers.
    • There is a delay through the AV node before it reaches the Bundle of His, so that the atria can contract, before the ventricles contract. The ventricles contract from the bottom up.

Delay of nodes - Conduction delay between AV node, and bundles on AV septum. Delay through the AV node of 90mS, and delay thorough the structural wall 40ms. total delay from arrival of action potential at the AV node (atria have been activated) and arrival at the bundle branch is about 130ms, gives times for atria to contract and empty blood into respective ventricles.

Main pacemaker in the heart is the SA node, the AV node will pick up if the SA node doesn't work. will work rhymthically but at a slower rate. The purkinje fibers can also fire rhytmically if there is a problem of conudction between AV and purkinje (more of a clinical issue).
